Problem

Commercial teams lacked data-driven methods to prioritize healthcare provider (HCP) outreach, resulting in inefficient resource allocation and suboptimal targeting strategies. Manual targeting approaches failed to identify high-potential HCPs likely to prescribe neuropsychiatric medications to new patients.

Solution

Built a gradient-boosted propensity scoring recommender system to identify and rank HCPs most likely to initiate new patients on neuropsychiatric medications. The system integrates prescription behavior, patient demographics, and treatment patterns from commercial data sources to generate actionable HCP recommendations for field teams.

Feature Selection Strategy

To ensure model robustness and minimize overfitting, implemented a dual model training approach:

  • Trained two independent gradient boosting models on different data splits
  • Extracted feature importance rankings from both models
  • Selected only features appearing in top rankings across both models (intersection analysis)
  • Validated feature stability across 50+ model iterations
  • Achieved ~90% overlap in top 10 features, ~80% in top 20, and ~75% in top 50

Implementation

Data Integration

Integrated prescription dispensing data (IQVIA SOB) with claims-level patient data (IQVIA LAAD) to construct comprehensive HCP behavioral profiles. Feature engineering translated domain hypotheses into quantitative signals across prescription patterns, patient demographics, treatment approaches, and market access characteristics.

Model Development

Implemented gradient boosting (XGBoost/LightGBM) with a dual model training strategy to ensure robust feature selection. Trained two independent models on different data partitions, then selected features appearing in top importance rankings across both models. This intersection approach minimized overfitting and improved generalization to unseen HCPs.

Explainability

Applied SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) analysis to generate individual HCP-level explanations for both high-propensity and low-propensity prescribers. These explanations enabled commercial teams to understand which behavioral patterns drove model predictions and identify hidden opportunities where low-scoring HCPs converted despite model predictions.

Validation Framework

Out-of-Time Backtesting: Validated model performance by training on historical periods and testing on future held-out periods the model had not seen. This temporal validation ensured the model could accurately predict HCP behavior in prospective deployment scenarios.

Prospective A/B Testing: Field validation methodology under development to measure real-world impact of model-driven targeting versus standard approaches.

The recommender system achieved strong precision-recall performance (0.77 PR-AUC) and demonstrated 5-7% lift in HCP conversion rates compared to baseline targeting approaches. Feature stability analysis across iterations showed ~90% consistency in top 10 drivers, ~80% in top 20, and ~75% in top 50, validating the robustness of the dual model selection strategy.

SHAP analysis revealed interpretable patterns: high-propensity HCPs exhibited specific combinations of specialty type, prior adoption behavior, and patient switching patterns. Analysis of low-propensity HCPs who nevertheless converted uncovered hidden opportunities and edge cases not captured by initial feature engineering, informing subsequent model refinements.
